Overview

This tool simulates a polarized light manipulation experiment based on Freas et al. (2019, Journal of Comparative Physiology A). A linear polarizing filter is placed over a foraging ant at a feeder and rotated while she feeds. The strongest prediction in the simulator is the polarization-only case, where the ant's home vector is rotated by the same total filter-angle manipulation. Because desert ants can also use landscape information, the app also offers a blended cue-conflict prediction instead of assuming polarization always dominates.

The tool has two windows side by side. Window 1 is the sky dome — the view looking straight up from the ground. Window 2 is a top-down ground view showing the ant's movement. Both windows are linked: dragging the nest or feeder in Window 1 updates Window 2 automatically.

Use the app for geometric experimental predictions under clear-sky Phoenix conditions, not as a full behavioral model. The sky polarization field is modeled with a Rayleigh-style single-scattering approximation, and the cue-conflict prediction depends on the polarization-versus-landscape weighting model you choose.

Although the simulator can be used more generally to think about cue conflict between polarized light and landscape information, its teaching notes and biological framing are designed mainly around Temnothorax rugatulus and related ant navigation questions.

How to use the tool — step by step

  • Set the date and time in Window 1 to match your planned field session (or use Manual mode to set the sun position directly). The sky color and E-vector pattern update to reflect the real sun position for Phoenix AZ.
  • Drag the nest dot (dark blue, labeled "Nest") on the sky canvas to any position inside the dome. This represents the nest's location relative to the sky reference frame.
  • Drag the feeder dot (green, labeled "Feeder") to set where the feeder is. The direction from nest to feeder is the ant's outbound heading. The filter circle appears at the feeder position.
  • Check the E-vector tick marks (blue lines) in and around the filter circle to see what polarization angle the ant will perceive at the feeder location.
  • Adjust the initial filter angle slider if you want the filter pre-rotated. At 0° the filter is near center alignment with the feeder's ambient E-vector — a green glow marks the control condition while the surrounding vectors show how close the whole filter field gets.
  • In Window 2, set the filter rotation during trial — this is the angle the filter will rotate while the ant feeds. Freas et al. used ±45°.
  • Set landscape rotation during trial if you want to model an artificial terrestrial panorama rotated relative to the true homeward direction.
  • Turn on landscape visualization if you want to see a cardboard-tube-style panorama ring around the feeder and watch it rotate during the manipulation.
  • Choose a compass cue model and set the animation speed.
  • Click Run experiment to watch the full trial: outbound walk → feeding pause → filter rotation → exit on error heading.
  • Use Reset to clear the trails and run again with different settings.

Window 1 — Sky dome controls

Date and time

Defaults to today's date and current hour in Phoenix MST (UTC−7, no daylight saving). A solar-position calculation tied to Phoenix latitude, longitude, and MST converts the selected date and time into sun azimuth and elevation. Adjust to match your planned field session. The sky color changes with sun elevation: bright blue at high elevation, warm orange near sunset/sunrise, deep navy during twilight.

Manual sun azimuth and elevation

Switch to "Manual az/el" mode to set the sun position directly using sliders rather than a date and time. Azimuth is the compass bearing of the sun (0° = north, 90° = east, 180° = south, 270° = west). Elevation is the height above the horizon in degrees (0° = horizon, 90° = directly overhead). The polarization pattern is strongest and most useful to ants at low sun elevations (0–20°), matching the twilight and pre-sunset conditions used in Freas et al.

Nest position — drag on sky canvas

The dark blue "Nest" dot on the sky canvas is fully draggable. Move it anywhere inside the dome circle. In the sky dome, the nest is at the center of the ant's reference frame — dragging it changes which part of the polarization pattern is overhead at the nest location. The feeder moves with it to preserve the trail direction. Window 2 mirrors this position automatically.

Feeder position — drag on sky canvas

The green "Feeder" dot is also freely draggable on the sky canvas. Drag it independently of the nest to set the feeder's azimuth and distance. The filter circle appears at the feeder location and shows the local E-vector angle at that point in the sky. The direction from nest to feeder is the ant's outbound heading, which becomes its homeward heading in reverse when it exits. Window 2 mirrors this position automatically.

Initial filter angle (−45° to +45°)

The orientation of the polarizing filter before the experiment trial begins. At 0°, the filter transmission axis is near center alignment with the ambient E-vector at the feeder location — this is the control condition, indicated by a green glow around the filter circle. Because the sky pattern changes across the filter area, the slits may be closer to some ambient vectors than others. Rotating it pre-shifts the E-vector the ant perceives before the trial rotation occurs. In most experiments this is left at 0°.

Filter opacity

Controls how visible the filter E-vector tick marks appear on the canvas. This is a display-only setting and does not affect the physics or the ant's behavior. Reduce it if the filter ticks are obscuring the ambient E-vector pattern underneath; increase it to make the filter manipulation more visible.

Window 2 — Experiment controls

Filter rotation during trial (−45° to +45°)

The number of degrees the filter physically rotates while the ant is feeding at the feeder. This is the core experimental manipulation. +45° rotates clockwise, −45° counterclockwise. In the polarization-only model, the ant's predicted home direction shifts by the total filter angle acting at the feeder. In the blended model, the shift is smaller because landscape information and polarization are averaged. Freas et al. used ±45° rotations in their manipulations.

Landscape rotation during trial (−180° to +180°)

Use this to model an artificial terrestrial panorama rotated relative to the ant's normal learned surroundings. In the landscape-only model, this rotation fully determines the predicted homeward shift. In the blended model, it competes with the manipulated polarization cue.

Landscape visualization

This optional bonus overlay draws a simple artificial panorama ring around the feeder, like a printed cardboard cylinder. When turned on, the ring rotates during the trial so you can visualize the terrestrial cue moving while the ant remains at the feeder.

Compass cue model

Polarization only shows the idealized case where the manipulated polarization cue fully determines the homeward heading. Landscape only shows the complementary case where the terrestrial panorama fully determines the homeward heading. Mixed cue shows a cue-conflict compromise in which the ant combines the rotated polarization cue with learned terrestrial panorama cues.

Animation speed

Ranges from 0.25× (very slow) through 1× (normal) to 5× (fast). Sub-1× speeds are especially useful for demonstrations — at 0.25× you can clearly see the filter E-vector lines rotate step by step and watch the ant's body reorient before it steps away from the feeder. Use faster speeds for quick trial previews when adjusting parameters.

Phase indicator

The four dots at the top of Window 2 light up as the animation progresses: Outbound (ant walks to feeder) → Feeding (ant pauses, filter in place) → Filter rotates (filter turns, ant reorients) → Exit (ant departs on shifted heading). Completed phases turn blue; the active phase is green.

Reading the sky dome

The dome is a view looking straight up. The center is the zenith (directly overhead) and the rim is the horizon. N, E, S, W are marked around the edge. The short blue tick marks show the local E-vector (polarization angle) across the sky — they run perpendicular to the direction toward the sun and form concentric arcs around it. The red dashed line is the solar meridian passing through the sun, zenith, and anti-solar point. The purple circle is the filter at the feeder location, with its own E-vector tick marks showing what the ant perceives through the filter.

The polarization pattern persists through twilight (sun 0° to −18° below the horizon), which is why ants continue to use it after sunset. During twilight the sky darkens but the E-vector ticks remain visible, and the sun is shown as a dashed circle at the horizon point.

Reading the angle diagram

The three circles at the bottom of Window 2 show E-vector orientation at the feeder location. The left circle (blue) shows the ambient E-vector without any filter. The middle circle (purple) shows the filter at its starting angle before the trial rotation. The right circle (red) shows the filter after rotation. In the polarization-only model, that total filter angle sets the full compass shift. In the blended model, it contributes one component of the final predicted heading alongside landscape information.

Tips for realistic field conditions

  • Set the time to early morning (6–8 AM) or late afternoon (4–6 PM) in November or April — these match the seasons and times used in Freas et al. (October–November 2018 and April 2019).
  • Sun elevation of 0–20° gives the strongest polarization pattern and is when V. pergandei is most active near the nest.
  • With feeder azimuth 90° (feeder due east of nest), the ant's homeward heading is 270° (west). In the polarization-only model, a total +45° filter manipulation predicts exit at 315° (NW); in the equal-weight polarization-plus-landscape model it predicts a smaller shift.
  • Drag the nest to a corner and the feeder toward the opposite side to maximize the visible trail length in Window 2.
  • Use 0.25× speed during presentations so the audience can see the filter rotate and the ant reorient before it exits.

What polarized skylight means

Sunlight reaching the top of the atmosphere is effectively unpolarized. After that light is Rayleigh scattered by air molecules, the scattered light becomes partially linearly polarized. At each point in the sky, one electric-field orientation dominates. That dominant orientation is called the e-vector orientation or angle of polarization.

The e-vector is perpendicular to the scattering plane, the plane defined by the observer, the Sun, and the point in the sky being viewed. In this simulator, the short blue bars show that local e-vector orientation across the sky dome.

Why the pattern is tied to the Sun

The skylight polarization pattern is not centered on the nest, the feeder, or the observer's walking direction. It is set by the solar geometry. As the Sun changes azimuth and elevation, the scattering angle to every point in the sky changes, and the polarization field changes with it.

In a single-scattering Rayleigh model, the degree of linear polarization is lowest near the Sun and anti-Sun and is strongest in a broad band about 90° from the Sun. That is why the pattern is often described as a sky-wide compass cue rather than a single arrow.

How the filter changes what the ant sees

A linear polarizing filter transmits one preferred e-vector orientation and attenuates the orthogonal one. In the lab setup represented here, the filter is placed over the ant at the feeder. The purple slit pattern shows the filter's transmission axis. Rotating the filter rotates the transmitted e-vector field that reaches the ant's dorsal rim area.

Because the ambient sky pattern varies across space, a finite circular filter cannot match every surrounding ambient e-vector perfectly at once. The important teaching comparison is the local relationship between the filter transmission axis and the ambient e-vector field in the immediate neighborhood of the feeder.

What happens at sunset, twilight, and night

After sunset, the Sun is below the horizon, but the upper atmosphere is still illuminated for a while. During civil, nautical, and astronomical twilight, a polarization pattern can still remain visible, although the sky gets darker and the signal generally becomes weaker and noisier than it is in full daylight.

At true night, when the Sun is well below the horizon, solar skylight polarization largely disappears. A similar but much weaker pattern can be produced by moonlight under clear conditions, because moonlight is reflected sunlight and is scattered by the atmosphere in the same general way. In practice, nighttime polarization cues are far weaker than daytime or twilight solar cues. This simulator is intended mainly for daytime and twilight solar polarization, not as a quantitative moonlight model.

Why ants can use it

Many insects, including desert ants, have a specialized dorsal rim area in the compound eye with photoreceptors tuned to polarization. Those receptors are sensitive to e-vector orientation rather than only brightness. In the insect brain, polarization-sensitive pathways converge on the central complex, which is strongly implicated in celestial compass coding and path-integration-related directional processing.

For the species modeled here, Temnothorax rugatulus, eye anatomy has been described in detail and the published figures include a dorsal rim area, so it is reasonable to say that this ant possesses the peripheral visual specializations expected for polarization sensitivity. What is less certain is how strongly this species relies on skylight polarization in the exact way desert ants such as Cataglyphis do, so the simulator should still be read as a mechanistic hypothesis tool rather than a species-complete neural model.

That is why a rotated filter can produce a predictable heading error: if the ant interprets the filtered e-vector field as a compass cue, its calculated homeward direction shifts toward the manipulated polarization direction. How large that shift is depends on how strongly the ant weights polarization relative to other available cues, including landscape information.

Landscape navigation and the ant brain

For learned visual navigation, the current evidence points less to a cortex-like map and more to interactions between the insect optic lobes, mushroom bodies, and central complex. Ants are thought to use stored panoramic scene memories to judge whether a current view is familiar and whether it is aligned with a learned route or nest direction.

The term retinocortical map is usually not used for ants, because they do not have a cortex. Some visual pathways in insects are retinotopically organized at early stages such as the optic lobes, but the navigation literature for ants more often discusses view-based memory, mushroom-body-dependent visual learning, and central-complex heading computation. So if you want a teaching section beyond polarized light, the most accurate framing would be something like Landscape navigation and scene memory, not retinocortical mapping.

One species-specific caution: a commonly cited visual-surround rotation study was originally published under Temnothorax rugatulus but was later corrected to Temnothorax curvispinosus. So it is fair to say that Temnothorax ants and ants more broadly can use landscape information, but that particular experiment should not be used as direct rugatulus-specific evidence.
