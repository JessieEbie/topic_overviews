From raw run to reviewed table.

This part keeps the processing plan next to the interface. With Agilent data, start with MassHunter or ChemStation exports now, then add raw `.D` conversion and deconvolution when you are ready.

Accepted in this prototype: CSV or TSV tables with columns like retention time, area, name, formula, class, and spectrum.

Agilent options

1
Quick report export.

Export a compound, peak, or library-search report from MassHunter or ChemStation as CSV, TXT, or XLSX. This is the easiest way to start reviewing peaks in the interface.

2
Spectrum-rich export.

If your Agilent report can include major ions, apex spectra, match score, formula, library name, and retention time, export those too. This gives the guide enough evidence to explain class patterns.

3
Raw `.D` conversion.

For full processing outside Agilent, convert the Agilent `.D` data folder to an open format such as mzML or NetCDF. That unlocks automated chromatogram reading, peak picking, and deconvolution.

4
Retention-index workflow.

Run an alkane ladder and calculate retention indices. RI patterns are especially helpful for long-chain alkanes, methyl-branched alkanes, alkenes, and esters.

5
Review and annotate.

Use the chromatogram, spectrum, class guide, and notes to decide whether a peak is a class-level call, a tentative compound, or confirmed.

What to export from Agilent first

  • Required: retention time, peak area or height, and candidate compound name.
  • Very helpful: library match score, formula, CAS/library ID, and major ions or apex spectrum.
  • Best for cuticle work: retention index, alkane ladder run, sample ID, and notes about blanks or contaminants.

CSV shape this tool understands

Column names are flexible. These examples are recognized: rt, retention_time, area, intensity, name, formula, class, match, spectrum.

The spectrum column can look like this:

57:100 71:66 85:42 99:24 113:12 408:7

What patterns are saying.

The guide is intentionally written for review work: not a perfect identification oracle, but a set of clues that helps you explain why a peak looks like an alkane, branched alkane, alkene, ester, or something else.

Cuticular hydrocarbons first

Ant cuticles are often dominated by long-chain hydrocarbons. Many peaks form homologous series, so a compound call should usually combine the spectrum, retention time, retention index, and neighboring peaks.

n-AlkanesStrong alkyl fragments such as m/z 57, 71, 85, 99. Molecular ion may be weak but helps estimate carbon number.
Methyl-branched alkanesStill hydrocarbon-like, but branch cleavage can enhance diagnostic fragments. Exact branch position is hard without standards or special methods.
AlkenesOften stronger m/z 55, 69, 83, 97 series. Double-bond position usually needs derivatization such as DMDS or standards.
EstersLook for oxygen-containing behavior, McLafferty ions such as m/z 74 for methyl esters, and acylium fragments such as m/z 43.

How to avoid over-calling

  • Class-level ID: enough evidence for hydrocarbon, alkene, ester, or contaminant, but not enough for exact structure.
  • Tentative compound: library hit and retention behavior agree, but no authentic standard.
  • Probable compound: high-quality spectrum, plausible RI, expected homologous context, and no stronger alternative.
  • Confirmed: authentic standard or independent structural confirmation.

Practical clue table

Odd/even chain seriesCuticular hydrocarbons often show repeating peaks spaced by one CH2 unit. Repeating RI spacing supports homologous assignments.
Weak molecular ionCommon in long alkanes. A weak high-mass ion can still anchor carbon number if the spectrum is clean.
Oxygenated peaksEsters, alcohols, ketones, and aldehydes may have stronger polar fragments and different retention behavior from hydrocarbons of similar mass.
CoelutionA mixed spectrum can imitate “other things I don’t know.” If ions do not rise and fall together, deconvolution or manual extraction may be needed.
