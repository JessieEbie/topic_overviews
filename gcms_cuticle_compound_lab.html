What to export from Agilent first

  • Required: retention time, peak area or height, and candidate compound name.
  • Very helpful: library match score, formula, CAS/library ID, and major ions or apex spectrum.
  • Best for cuticle work: retention index, alkane ladder run, sample ID, and notes about blanks or contaminants.

What the app will actually do

  • With a report table: it will load the listed peaks directly and let you review them one by one.
  • With a raw `DATA.MS` file: it will build a total ion chromatogram and detect approximate peaks from the raw scans.
  • What it will not do automatically: prove exact branch position, prove double-bond position, or replace authentic standards.

What patterns are saying

This guide is for real review work. It is not a magic identification button. Use it to decide what class a peak most likely belongs to, what evidence supports that call, and where you should stay cautious.

Cuticular hydrocarbons first

Ant cuticles are often dominated by long-chain hydrocarbons. Many peaks form homologous series, so a compound call should usually combine the spectrum, retention time, retention index, and neighboring peaks.

n-AlkanesLook for a 14 u fragment series such as 57, 71, 85, 99, 113. In long-chain CHCs the pattern often looks like an envelope or clustered hump, not a perfect staircase.
Methyl-branched alkanesStill hydrocarbon-like, but alpha-cleavage ions on each side of the branch can help. Use retention index and literature patterns together, because many positional isomers remain ambiguous.
AlkenesOften emphasize allylic ions such as 55, 69, 83, 97. Think “unsaturation-shifted hydrocarbon pattern,” not one fixed bimodal shape. Double-bond position usually needs DMDS or standards.
EstersLook for oxygen-containing behavior, McLafferty ions such as m/z 74 for methyl esters, and acylium fragments such as m/z 43.

How to avoid over-calling

  • Class-level ID: enough evidence for hydrocarbon, alkene, ester, or contaminant, but not enough for exact structure.
  • Tentative compound: library hit and retention behavior agree, but no authentic standard.
  • Probable compound: high-quality spectrum, plausible RI, expected homologous context, and no stronger alternative.
  • Confirmed: authentic standard or independent structural confirmation.

Practical clue table

Odd/even chain seriesCuticular hydrocarbons often show repeating peaks spaced by one CH2 unit. Repeating RI spacing supports homologous assignments.
Weak molecular ionCommon in long alkanes. A weak high-mass ion can still anchor carbon number if the spectrum is clean.
Oxygenated peaksEsters, alcohols, ketones, and aldehydes may have stronger polar fragments and different retention behavior from hydrocarbons of similar mass.
CoelutionA mixed spectrum can imitate “other things I don’t know.” If ions do not rise and fall together, deconvolution or manual extraction may be needed.

How to think about methyl branch position

  • First decide whether it is branched at all. The spectrum should still look hydrocarbon-like, but some fragments will be stronger than expected for a straight-chain alkane.
  • Then look for paired alpha-cleavage hints. Cleavage on each side of the methyl branch can create a pair of diagnostic ions. Complementary pairs are more informative than a single isolated peak.
  • Use retention index as a second test. Branch position assignments are much stronger when the fragmentation pattern and RI both point to the same region of the chain.
  • Expect ambiguity. Internal branch positions often cannot be separated cleanly. In real CHC tables, it is common to report slash notation such as 12-/13-/14-MeC28 when EI and RI narrow the field but do not prove a single isomer.

CHC molecular-weight table

Methyl-branched alkanes have the same formula and molecular weight as the straight-chain alkane with the same total carbon number. The table uses average molecular weight and shows the nominal molecular ion in parentheses.

Use this as a quick carbon-number check, not as a final identification. Retention index, spectrum quality, and standards still matter.

2. How a hydrocarbon makes a fragment pattern

A long alkane is mostly carbon and hydrogen. Under EI conditions, it can break at many carbon-carbon bonds. Those breaks create a family of alkyl fragments that differ by one CH2 group, which is 14 mass units. In real long-chain CHC spectra, this often appears as an envelope or clustered pattern rather than a perfectly even staircase.

3. Read a spectrum in five passes

  • Find the base peak. This is the tallest bar. For hydrocarbons it is often m/z 57, 55, 69, 71, or a related fragment.
  • Look for a series. Alkanes often show 43, 57, 71, 85, 99, 113 with 14 u spacing. Alkenes often still look hydrocarbon-like, but the emphasis shifts toward allylic ions such as 55, 69, 83, 97.
  • Check the molecular-ion region. A small high-mass peak can help with carbon number, but it may be weak in long CHCs.
  • Compare retention time. Longer hydrocarbon chains usually come out later. A sudden odd retention time can mean coelution or a different class.
  • Decide the confidence level. Class-level is okay. Do not force an exact structure when branch position or double-bond position is not supported.

5. Class-by-class beginner clues

n-Alkane

Look for 14 u spacing in a hydrocarbon-like cluster: often 43, 57, 71, 85, 99, 113. Retention time should fit the chain-length series.

Methyl-branched alkane

Still looks hydrocarbon-like, but branch-local alpha-cleavage pairs can strengthen certain fragments. Report branch position cautiously unless standards, RI, or strong literature support it.

Alkene

Look for stronger 55, 69, 83, 97 ions and a hydrocarbon pattern that is shifted toward allylic fragments. Do not rely on one exact “bimodal” look. The double-bond position usually still needs derivatization or standards.

Ester

For methyl esters, m/z 74 is a useful clue. Oxygenated compounds often retain differently from hydrocarbons with similar size.

6. A safe annotation habit

Write the evidence, not just the name. A good note might say: "Hydrocarbon series with m/z 57/71/85/99, weak M+ near 380, retention near C27 standard, probable n-heptacosane." A cautious note might say: "Alkene-like 55/69/83 pattern, exact double-bond position not assigned."
